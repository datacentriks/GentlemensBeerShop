03 · Identity Mark

Logo Usage

The logo mark is a hop cone arranged as a beard, topped with a bowler hat — a visual pun merging "gentlemen" (hat) with craft beer (hops). The Christmas variant replaces the bowler hat with a Santa hat for seasonal use only. Both marks are available in full-colour, white (reversed), and black (monochrome) versions.

  • ✅
    Do: Maintain clear space Always surround the mark with minimum clear space equal to the width of one "O" from the wordmark.
  • ✅
    Do: Use approved colour variants Full colour on cream/white, reversed white on dark green, monochrome on single-colour applications.
  • ❌
    Don't: Stretch or skew Never distort the proportions of the logo mark or wordmark.
  • ❌
    Don't: Use Christmas variant year-round The Santa hat version is seasonal (November–January) only.
  • ❌
    Don't: Place on busy backgrounds The logo must always be legible. Use a colour-fill background panel if needed.
  • ❌
    Don't: Recreate the logo in text Always use the provided vector files. Never attempt to typeset the logo.
04 · Photography Style

Visual Language

Photography is the primary storytelling tool. The style is warm, editorial, and product-led. Natural light is strongly preferred. Backgrounds are minimal — let the beer labels and glassware speak.

✓ Photography: Do
  • Use warm, natural golden-hour light
  • Shoot with shallow depth-of-field for atmosphere
  • Emphasise the label art and can design
  • Include branded glassware (logo on glass) when possible
  • Use minimal, clean backgrounds (wood, marble, linen)
  • Show the colour of the beer through the glass
  • Allow creative props (chess pieces, books) sparingly
✗ Photography: Don't
  • Use harsh, flat flash or overhead artificial light
  • Show messy or cluttered environments
  • Crop labels so they're unreadable
  • Use heavy saturation or HDR processing
  • Use stock photography for product shots
  • Include people's faces without proper consent
  • Mix beer from other brands without curation context
05 · Tone & Voice

Brand Voice

The Gentlemen's Beer Shop knows its beer — and isn't afraid to show it. The tone is knowledgeable without being a lecture, playful without being silly, and warm without being generic.

Knowledgeable

We have genuine expertise. We reference specific breweries, styles, ingredients, and production methods — accurately and with confidence.

"Clock Brewery's IPA Zoom is a showcase of Simcoe and Citra hops — clean bitterness, zero compromise."
✗ Not: "A delicious craft beer you'll love!"

Playful

We don't take ourselves too seriously. The hop-beard mascot sets the tone — intelligent but self-aware, with room for humour.

"Yes, there are 47 IPAs on the shelf. No, we don't think that's a problem."
✗ Not: "Experience our world-class establishment."

Curated

Everything we carry is chosen deliberately. Language should convey selection and discernment — we're editors, not a warehouse.

"We only stock beers we'd personally drink. If it's here, it earned its place."
✗ Not: "We have everything for everyone."

Local

We're in Bratislava. We speak Slovak and English. Local references, local pride — without being parochial.

"Otvárame o 14:00. Príďte skôr, kým vypredáme novinky." (bilingual OK)
✗ Not: Generic corporate-English-only copy.

Warm

We're a shop you come back to. The language should feel like a recommendation from a friend who knows their beer — never cold or transactional.

"Not sure what to try? Tell us what you liked last time and we'll find something better."
✗ Not: "Browse our extensive product catalogue."

Direct

No padding. No filler. Craft beer people appreciate precision. Get to the point — style, brewery, what makes it worth trying.

"Pastry stout. 11%. Tastes like a brownie made a deal with a hop farmer. Limited to 12 bottles."
✗ Not: "Indulge in an amazing sensory journey..."
